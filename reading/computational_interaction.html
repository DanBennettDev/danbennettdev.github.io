Computational Interaction & Intention

Two of the most significant influences on my thinking this year have come at least in part from Antti Oulasvirta. First came his paper with Kaspar Hornbaek What is Interaction and then a book he co-edited: Computational Interaction, and a linked summer-school in Cambridge, which I attended.

Computational Interaction is the flagship textbook for a new approach to HCI research, which places a stronger emphasis on cognitive modelling and conventional computer science methods and patterns. The book is a great resource for any HCI researcher, and a substantial overview and introduction to the Computational approach. It introduces the HCI researcher techniques from combinatorial optimisation, information theory, machine learning, and cognitive and economic modelling.

A major thrust of the Computational Interaction movement seems to be a desire to move towards more quantifiable measures of interaction, approaches more amenable to computational techniques. This is in the context of HCI research increasingly moving towards less-empirical design-driven approaches, ethnography, and increasingly influenced by holistic philosophies from thinkers as disparate, but equally non-quantifying as Heidegger and Latour. Computational Interaction wishes to move to a more theory-building approach, emphasising the proposing and testing of hypotheses, and the generation of concepts which can determine predictions and designs.

Some people I have talked to see What is Interaction as laying an ideological groundwork for Computational Interaction movement. I am not so convinced - I don’t think much of Kaspar Hornbaek’s work can be squashed into the Computational Interaction mould. Also, while the paper clearly makes space for Computational approaches in the HCI landscape, the approach here is far broader than that. One way in which the paper connects to Computational Interaction is in the emphasis on theory building and hypothesis testing. What Is Interaction gives some hints towards a broad proposal for an improved HCI methodology, building on Dubin’s account of Theory Building, but importantly, this is a very broad church trying to accommodate design perspectives as well as approaches building on Heidegger and ecological psychology. For me, one of the most important points in the paper is something which I actually think poses a problem for Computational Interaction. The authors give a very clear articulation of an issue I have been thinking about for a while - the way that accounts of technology often treat humans as more-or-less static, Cartesian, mind-body systems. A pre-formed intention which can be inferred through signals transmitted to the computer via the body. The paper argues that HCI must develop approaches which treat intention as dynamic during interaction. To explain why I think this an issue for CI, I’ll need to talk about Computational Interaction a little more.

Reductive?

Despite having read my share of Heidegger, and having suggested I see a problem for Computational Interaction, I am pretty much on board with it as an approach to HCI. There is some very cool, useful research in this area, and I really like the drive to give HCI more rigorous foundations. I know in some quarters, though, there is a suspicion that a Computational Interaction attitude will diminish our focus on context, and general human complexity. Perhaps this comes from memories of the way that narrowly behaviourist approaches in mid-century psychology gave us a distorted picture of the human, and tended to be combative and ideologically critical of what turned out to be incredibly effective approaches in ecological and positive psychology. To date I think this is unfounded - the major proponents of Computational Interaction seem pragmatic and open. As I say What Is Interaction, seems to me very pluralist (maybe to a fault - I personally don’t see how you integrate an honestly Heidegger-derived embodied perspective into the framework without seriously changing one or the other. But then I would personally like to see less Heidegger in HCI.).

I don’t entirely agree with these worries. If some think Computational approaches to Interaction might prove to be de-contextualising and de-humanising, my attraction to them follows from an opposite instinct. I see potential here for systems to respond more effectively to issues of human well-being. I see an opportunity to diminish centrality of control, build systems which adapt better to context and which make user-customisation, and adaptation possible without unsustainable developer effort. I see a constructive approach which can be allied to the critical techniques in other areas in HCI.

Dynamics of Intention

At the same time I do see some reason for caution. An illustration of this comes in a twitter thread by Antti Oulasvirta. The thread is incredibly interesting, and there’s a lot I really like in it, but I also think it demonstrates the issue around static/ dynamic intention which, I noted above, Oulasvirta himself raised in his paper What Is Interaction. In the thread, Oulasvirta discusses the way that we are locked in to reductive, controlling interface designs, which diminish the ability of users to progress from novices to expert users. The general idea here is that novice-users prefer incremental modes of interaction: splitting a large task into a series of small actions each of which gives feedback. In contrast to this, an ideal expert user would use a far more efficient approach, not reliant on repetition of action-feedback cycles, but which would shortcut to the immediate execution of the intended action. The argument goes that our reliance on designs which serve the former approach prevent us from developing really efficient, effective interfaces, that allow more direct execution of users’ intentions.

The point here is well made, and I think has a lot of truth to it. However, I think the diagnosis is partial, and its overemphasis would risk diminishing the role of something irreducibly complex and important: We often do not come to technologies with our intentions fully formed. Instead we form our intentions through tool use, in dialogue with the tool. This is not only true of novice users. In fact research has shown that in some kinds of interaction, expert users actually make more use of “inefficient”, fast-feedback interaction than novices. Increasingly, research shows that much cognition takes place via action, in complex feedback with the tools we use. The problem for some existing modelling approaches is that cognition may be far more difficult to model if intention is dynamic, than if it is treated as pre-formed. As such Computational Interaction techniques may diminish emphasis on interactive-cognition, by modelling intent as something static. Quite a few of the computational techniques I see in the book (and many of those I saw at the computational interaction summer school) lean heavily on the idea of clear and pre-established intention in the user. I’m not the only person to worry about such things - researchers at Cambridge (a centre of Computational Interaction thinking and host of this years Computational Interaction Summer School) produced this paper outlining issues around the intersection of computational approaches and inconvenient complexity.

I should finish by saying that I don’t think any of this is a necessary feature of a Computational approach - only that the ease of modelling with static intentions might lead computational work to de-emphasise dynamics of intention, and that this might be problematic. It is worth noting that there are many interaction situations where intentional dynamics will be of relatively minimal concern, and which will be well modelled by treating intention as effectively static. Nothing I say above diminishes the value of Computational Interaction techniques in those situations. Finally, I started this piece by pointing out that Antti Oulasvirta, an architect of Computational Interaction, was also a co-author on What is Interaction which pointed to HCI’s failure to account for intention-formation and change during interaction. Oulasvirta for one is clearly thinking about these same issues, so we can hope that computational interaction will increasingly develop approaches which address the dynamics of intention.
